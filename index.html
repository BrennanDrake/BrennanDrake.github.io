SLAM and ROS for Planogram Compliance

Undergraduate Thesis


During my undergrad at Georgia Southern University, with the help of my faculty mentor Dr. Alba Flores, I independently pursued robotics research. In reflection, my work was a tremendous amount of self study and literature review but was fundamental to fostering my passion for robotics and in giving me a solid foundation for growth. Through published works, online resources, and textbooks, I was able to teach myself about ROS1 and ROS2 architecture, how a complex system can be discretized, and extensively about Simultaneous Localization and Mapping.

As can be seen in my work below, using a Turtlebot3 system, I fabricated an experimental assembly to integrate PN5180 NFC/RFID reader data into the turtlebot's control system. By the conclusion of my studies, I had completed the experimental assembly with detected RFID tag IDs being published to a ROS topic, and had validated this collection with a small script of motion prompted by specific IDs. I would like to perform more work focused on this field, pertaining to visual SLAM methods, integration of LLM and object recognition, and higher level AI and and machine learning topics.

Photos and videos of the project can be seen in the portfolio section above.

Monsters, Inc Echo Mount

In this project, I wanted to create an interesting mount for my amazon alexa dot. In doing so, I decided to create a replica Scream Canister from Monsters Inc and with the help of some microcontrollers I was able to sync light and sounds.

This project tested my abilities in circuit design and allowed me to grow in my coding skills as well. I prototyped my circuit in EasyEDA, physically using cardboard and solder, and then finally with a fabricated pcb from JLC Pcb. I utilized a raspbery pi zero w for the brains and an array of transistors to allow for staggered lighting of the LEDs in sync with an audio file I extracted from Monster's University.

  • Skills Used Most: CAD Design, 3D printing, 3D printed finishing, circuit prototyping and assembly, and multithreaded Python coding.
  • Tools and Software: CR-10 Mini 3D printer, OnShape CAD Software (Free to college and highschool students), EasyEDA circuit design, Spyder Python IDE, Adobe Audition, Soldering Iron, Sanding Tools, Bondo, and Spraypaint

Thesis Document Found In "Research" Section

At Georgia Southern University, I attended to my Bachelors in Mechanical Engineering studies while a member of the Honors college. As a member I pursued undergraduate research focusing on robotics. I had previously become interested in the Robot Operating System, and sought to explore the use of static RFID/NFC landmarks in a Simultaneous Localization and Mapping process.

As can be seen in my paper below, the entent of my work at the time was the creation of the experimental assembly and a comprehensive literature search. The focus on this search was narrowed on lidar based SLAM and integrating PN5180 reader RFID data into existing packages such as GMapping.

The experimental assembly was fabricated to integrate with a Turtlebot3 "Burger" configuration. This assembly included structures modeled after the plates used on the turtlebot and 3d printed, logic level conversion circuits fabricated by hand on protoboards, a fuse assembly, an Arduino Uno, a step-down converter to power the assembly from the same battery as the robot, and a fuse cradle for circuit protection.
